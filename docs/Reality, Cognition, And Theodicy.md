2. Cognitive Structure and the Categories of Experience

Cognition is necessarily structured. Human experience is intelligible only through fundamental categories such as time, change, causality, and spatial extension. 3. Time, Cosmological Finitude, and the Question of Origins

Modern cosmology strongly suggests that the universe has a finite temporal past, most notably through Big Bang cosmology (Hawking & Ellis, The Large Scale Structure of Space-Time). If time itself has a beginning, then Reality—at least in its physical manifestation—cannot be eternal in a temporal sense.

Classical metaphysics rejects the notion that something can arise from absolute nothingness (ex nihilo nihil fit). Even the theological doctrine of creation ex nihilo does not posit nothingness as a causal principle, but rather affirms God as the sustaining source of being (Augustine, Confessions, XI). The beginning of time therefore represents not the emergence of being from nothing, but the transition from a non-temporal or meta-temporal mode of existence into temporal actuality.

4. The Reality Principle in the CTMU

Chris Langan’s Cognitive-Theoretical Model of the Universe proposes that Reality is a self-contained, self-configuring system governed by what he terms the Reality Principle (Langan, "The Cognitive-Theoretic Model of the Universe"). According to this principle, Reality is both the totality of existence and the logical framework that describes itself.

In the CTMU, Reality functions as a form of self-executing syntax: the laws of physics are not externally imposed but internally selected constraints necessary for global self-consistency. This model collapses the traditional subject–object dichotomy by asserting that Reality itself is intrinsically cognitive. Mind and world are thus not ontologically disjoint, but mutually implicative.

5. Unbound Telesis and the Rejection of Absolute Nothingness

Central to the CTMU is the concept of Unbound Telesis (UBT), an infinite and unrestricted potential for self-realization. UBT is not nothingness, but rather the absence of constraint. From this perspective, absolute nothingness is metaphysically incoherent, because the potential for existence already constitutes a form of being.

Reality emerges when UBT self-selects constraints, actualizing a determinate universe. This process is teleological rather than mechanistic: existence occurs because self-consistency demands expression. In this respect, the CTMU aligns with classical arguments that privilege being over non-being, while reframing them in informational and cognitive terms.

6. Aristotle, Augustine, and the First Cause

Aristotle’s argument for a Prime Mover begins with the empirical reality of change. Since nothing can actualize its own potential without an already actual cause, there must exist an Unmoved Mover that initiates motion without itself being moved (Metaphysics, XII). This Prime Mover is pure actuality, devoid of potentiality.

Augustine incorporates this framework into Christian theology by identifying the Prime Mover with a timeless, immutable God (Augustine, City of God, XI). Creation does not represent a change in God, but the origin of temporal reality itself. This account allows God to function as First Cause without being subject to temporal regress.

7. The Problem of Evil and Divine Culpability

The Problem of Evil challenges the coherence of classical theism by asserting a tension between divine omnipotence, omnibenevolence, and the existence of evil. Thomas Aquinas famously responds by denying that evil has positive ontological status, defining it instead as a privation of good (Summa Theologiae, I.48). On this account, God does not create evil; evil arises through the limitations and failures of finite agents exercising free will.

However, this privation theory does not fully absolve God of responsibility if God is understood, in Aristotelian–Augustinian terms, as the First Cause of all that exists. If every finite cause derives its causal power from God, then God remains the ultimate source of the causal chains that culminate in evil acts or states of affairs. Even if evil lacks substantive being, the conditions under which privations occur are intentionally instantiated by God. As First Cause, God selects the total structure of Reality, including the metaphysical possibility of moral failure.

Thus, while God may not be the proximate cause of evil, God is arguably the ultimate cause in a hierarchical sense. The appeal to secondary causation mitigates but does not eliminate divine culpability, because God freely chooses to actualize a world in which evil is possible and, given divine omniscience (on classical theism), foreknown. The distinction between permission and causation becomes morally tenuous when applied to an omnipotent being who could have actualized alternative realities without such privations.

This reformulation does not imply that God is morally evil, but it does require revising traditional theodicies. Divine goodness must be understood not as the absence of responsibility for evil, but as a willingness to bear ultimate responsibility for a Reality whose goods are inseparable from the risks of moral and natural suffering. Any adequate theodicy must therefore confront, rather than evade, the implication that First Causality entails ultimate moral accountability.

8. Determinism, Indeterminism, and Self-Determinism in a Temporally Structured Universe

Debates over determinism and indeterminism are often framed as mutually exclusive alternatives. Determinism holds that all events are necessitated by antecedent causes in accordance with fixed laws of nature, while indeterminism allows for genuine contingency and probabilistic outcomes. Classical Newtonian physics strongly supported determinism, most famously articulated by Laplace’s vision of an intellect that could predict the entire future given complete knowledge of present conditions (Laplace, Essai philosophique sur les probabilités). By contrast, quantum mechanics introduces irreducible indeterminacy at fundamental scales, particularly in the behavior of particles below the Planck length (Heisenberg, Physics and Philosophy).

Within the framework developed in this paper, however, the determinism–indeterminism dichotomy is incomplete. If Reality emerges from Unbound Telesis as a self-selecting, self-constraining system, then the universe is neither wholly determined by prior external causes nor radically indeterminate in a chaotic sense. Rather, it is more accurately described as self-deterministic. Its laws are not imposed from without, but arise from internal requirements of logical consistency, stability, and intelligibility.

At the quantum level, this self-determinism permits genuine openness. Indeterminacy reflects the universe’s freedom to explore multiple possible actualizations consistent with its internal constraints. This domain of probabilistic freedom is not lawless; it is bounded by mathematically precise rules governing allowable outcomes. Above the Planck scale, however, classical physics reliably governs macroscopic behavior. Causal regularities emerge as stable patterns, giving rise to the appearance of determinism necessary for coherent structures, persistent identities, and rational agency.

Thus, the universe simultaneously exhibits indeterminacy and determinacy at different levels of description, unified by an internally driven process of constraint selection. From a CTMU perspective, Reality continuously determines itself by narrowing possibilities into actuality without reference to external causation. This conception preserves the insights of both classical determinism and quantum indeterminism while avoiding their excesses. It also provides a metaphysical foundation for genuine freedom that does not collapse into randomness, supporting later claims about moral agency and divine responsibility.

This raises the unsettling possibility that actuality is not fully fixed prior to interaction with an observer, blurring the boundary between ontology (what exists) and epistemology (what is known).

10. Evil, Moral Agency, and Isolation: The Case of Alexander Selkirk

Moral evil is typically understood as relational, involving harm, injustice, or betrayal inflicted by one agent upon another. This raises the question of whether evil can meaningfully exist in conditions of extreme isolation. A historically concrete illustration of this problem is found in the case of Alexander Selkirk, the eighteenth-century sailor marooned alone on Más a Tierra (Robinson Crusoe Island) for over four years.

Selkirk’s isolation dramatically constrained the range of possible moral actions available to him. Deprived of social relations, institutions, and reciprocal obligations, he was incapable of committing many paradigmatic moral evils: he could not murder, defraud, oppress, deceive, or exploit others. The absence of other moral agents effectively severed the relational structures within which most sins acquire their meaning. While Selkirk could experience suffering, fear, despair, or negligence toward himself, such states are more plausibly categorized as prudential or psychological failures rather than moral evils in the strict sense.

This does not imply that Selkirk was morally inert. He retained limited forms of moral agency, such as stewardship over his environment, care for animals, and discipline over his own habits. He could cultivate virtues like patience and temperance, or vices such as sloth and despair. However, even these were attenuated, lacking the interpersonal dimension that ordinarily grounds moral obligation and culpability.

Selkirk’s case therefore illustrates that moral evil is not merely a matter of internal intention or abstract rule violation, but is fundamentally embedded in networks of social and cognitive relations. Isolation radically narrows the moral landscape, transforming many potential sins into logical impossibilities. This supports the broader claim that evil is not a mind-independent feature of Reality, but a relational phenomenon emergent among interacting cognizing agents.

11. Indeterminism, Limited Divine Foreknowledge, and Moral Judgment

If Reality is partially indeterministic, then the future cannot be exhaustively specified even in principle. Under such conditions, classical doctrines of complete divine foreknowledge encounter a moral difficulty rather than merely a logical one. If God infallibly knows every future evil prior to creation and nevertheless actualizes the precise conditions guaranteeing those evils, divine culpability becomes unavoidable. Limiting divine foreknowledge of contingent futures is therefore not a retreat from omniscience, but a morally motivated refinement of it.

On this view, God knows all that can be known: all necessary truths, all possibilities, and all presently actual states of affairs. What God does not know are future free contingents, not due to cognitive deficiency, but because such facts do not yet exist to be known. This position, associated with open theism and compatible with certain indeterministic interpretations of quantum mechanics, preserves both divine rationality and moral coherence.

Moral judgment, under these constraints, emerges dynamically through the lived experiences of cognizing agents. Good and evil are not arbitrarily assigned labels, nor are they eternally fixed outcomes embedded in creation from the outset. Rather, they arise relationally within an anthropically constrained universe in which freedom is genuine and outcomes are not pre-scripted. God’s evaluative judgments are thus responsive to actualized histories rather than predetermined scripts.

This model mitigates divine culpability by severing the link between First Causality and foreknown evil outcomes. God remains the ultimate ground of Reality and its possibilities, but not the author of specific evils that arise through genuinely free and indeterministic processes. Ethical responsibility is therefore distributed rather than centralized, preserving meaningful moral agency for creatures while maintaining divine goodness.


Conclusion

By integrating classical metaphysics, modern physics, and cognitive models of Reality, this paper argues that existence is fundamentally cognitive and teleological. Evil is neither metaphysically necessary nor illusory, but a contingent phenomenon arising within a temporal universe structured to permit cognition and freedom. Theodicy is therefore best addressed not by denying evil, but by situating it within a Reality whose deepest commitments are self-consistency, intelligibility, and moral agency.

Such a model would render freedom illusory and reintroduce divine culpability for evil outcomes. The philosophical provocation, therefore, is not that quantum mechanics proves theological claims, but that it provides a powerful analogy forcing reconsideration of long-standing assumptions about determinism, observation, and the moral implications of foreknowledge.

10. Evil, Moral Agency, and Isolation: The Case of Alexander Selkirk

Moral evil is typically understood as relational, involving harm, injustice, or betrayal inflicted by one agent upon another. This raises the question of whether evil can meaningfully exist in conditions of extreme isolation. A historically concrete illustration of this problem is found in the case of Alexander Selkirk, the eighteenth-century sailor marooned alone on Más a Tierra (Robinson Crusoe Island) for over four years.

Selkirk’s isolation dramatically constrained the range of possible moral actions available to him. Deprived of social relations, institutions, and reciprocal obligations, he was incapable of committing many paradigmatic moral evils: he could not murder, defraud, oppress, deceive, or exploit others. The absence of other moral agents effectively severed the relational structures within which most sins acquire their meaning. While Selkirk could experience suffering, fear, despair, or negligence toward himself, such states are more plausibly categorized as prudential or psychological failures rather than moral evils in the strict sense.

This does not imply that Selkirk was morally inert. He retained limited forms of moral agency, such as stewardship over his environment, care for animals, and discipline over his own habits. He could cultivate virtues like patience and temperance, or vices such as sloth and despair. However, even these were attenuated, lacking the interpersonal dimension that ordinarily grounds moral obligation and culpability.

Selkirk’s case therefore illustrates that moral evil is not merely a matter of internal intention or abstract rule violation, but is fundamentally embedded in networks of social and cognitive relations. Isolation radically narrows the moral landscape, transforming many potential sins into logical impossibilities. This supports the broader claim that evil is not a mind-independent feature of Reality, but a relational phenomenon emergent among interacting cognizing agents.

11. Indeterminism, Limited Divine Foreknowledge, and Moral Judgment

If Reality is partially indeterministic, then the future cannot be exhaustively specified even in principle. Under such conditions, classical doctrines of complete divine foreknowledge encounter a moral difficulty rather than merely a logical one. If God infallibly knows every future evil prior to creation and nevertheless actualizes the precise conditions guaranteeing those evils, divine culpability becomes unavoidable. Limiting divine foreknowledge of contingent futures is therefore not a retreat from omniscience, but a morally motivated refinement of it.

On this view, God knows all that can be known: all necessary truths, all possibilities, and all presently actual states of affairs. What God does not know are future free contingents, not due to cognitive deficiency, but because such facts do not yet exist to be known. This position, associated with open theism and compatible with certain indeterministic interpretations of quantum mechanics, preserves both divine rationality and moral coherence.

Moral judgment, under these constraints, emerges dynamically through the lived experiences of cognizing agents. Good and evil are not arbitrarily assigned labels, nor are they eternally fixed outcomes embedded in creation from the outset. Rather, they arise relationally within an anthropically constrained universe in which freedom is genuine and outcomes are not pre-scripted. God’s evaluative judgments are thus responsive to actualized histories rather than predetermined scripts.

This model mitigates divine culpability by severing the link between First Causality and foreknown evil outcomes. God remains the ultimate ground of Reality and its possibilities, but not the author of specific evils that arise through genuinely free and indeterministic processes. Ethical responsibility is therefore distributed rather than centralized, preserving meaningful moral agency for creatures while maintaining divine goodness.


Conclusion

By integrating classical metaphysics, modern physics, and cognitive models of Reality, this paper argues that existence is fundamentally cognitive and teleological. Evil is neither metaphysically necessary nor illusory, but a contingent phenomenon arising within a temporal universe structured to permit cognition and freedom. Theodicy is therefore best addressed not by denying evil, but by situating it within a Reality whose deepest commitments are self-consistency, intelligibility, and moral agency.
